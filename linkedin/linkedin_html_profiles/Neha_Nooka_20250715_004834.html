About

I hate selling myself. So, here’s quick things about me and my work. In, out, nobody gets hurt.

I take great pride in persistence, diligence, and empathy. I educate, refine and drive myself to be a better person. I’m that person at college/office that everyone wants to know but nobody wants to become. If you want to get something done, you come to me! Yet no one wants to do what I do because of the amount of personal time and effort that goes into being everyone’s go-to. I’ll bend over backwards to help others’ problems, but I also understand the importance of standing your ground and owning your own issues.

As a Graduate Research Assistant at University of Maryland Baltimore County (UMBC), I work on assessing the activities and impacts of the Center for Women in Technology (CWIT). I manage and organize data, develop reports and visualizations, and document data collection and analysis procedures. I also help the CWIT staff plan and implement long-term strategies for data management.

I am pursuing my Master of Science in Data Science at UMBC, where I also serve as a Graduate Teaching Assistant for the course DATA 690: Introduction to Natural Language Processing (NLP). I tutor and grade students in this course, and I have proficiency in NLP and data analysis skills. I have also earned multiple Microsoft certifications in security, compliance, identity, Azure, and Power Platform fundamentals.

I am passionate about learning new things and exploring different domains of data science. I have a background in information technology, and I have worked as an app developer and a web developer before. I am always looking for new challenges and opportunities to grow and contribute. I love meeting new people and hearing new perspectives. Please feel free to contact me via LinkedIn for an insightful conversation.

    New teachers face significant cognitive overload in visualizing and understanding the multiplicity of factors influencing teaching and learning beyond curriculum. This study evaluates the effectiveness of virtual reality (VR) data visualizations in aiding novice teachers to understand and connect these factors more efficiently. Through the use of geo-referenced quantitative data on historical, economic, environmental, and sociocultural factors, combined with qualitative data comprised of community asset mapping and interviews with community members, novice teachers are oriented to the community context of their school in a novel way that disrupts deficit orientations. This study aims to pioneer an innovative approach to data visualization that spurs new pedagogical approaches and educational possibilities.
